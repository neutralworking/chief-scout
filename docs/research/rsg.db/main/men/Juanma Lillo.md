
Juanma Lillo revolutionizes the tactical section with novel approaches The Gipuzkoa coach, after a dazzling start on the benches, seeks to relaunch his career in a Real Sociedad surrounded by problems.

24·10·08|00:00

At 42 years old, Juan Manuel Lillo (Tolosa, Guipúzcoa, 11/3/1965) does not know how to ride a bicycle. When he was a child, they wanted to give him one, but he didn't want to. He didn't learn due to lack of time. Everyone I had was for the ball.

At the age of 17 he took the youth Amaroz, a team from a neighborhood in his city. 
In a row, he went to the Tolosa youth team, regrading him to the national category without losing a match. He achieved it without any degree (he obtained the youth card in 1986).

Lillo continued there, but, in the middle of the campaign, he took over the first team, which was evicted... and managed to save him. He returned to the youth, and again triumphed in a big way: runner-up in the League, after Real Sociedad. He then took the regional title. 

For the first time, he left Guipúzcoa to lead Mirandés, in 3ª. 
Another success: he promoted him, being the highest scorer and the least beaten. 
He obtained the national title at the age of 24. 
At that age and, during the _military_, he was appointed professor of the National School of Coaches. 
He returned to Mirandés, who was still in 2ª B, but they wanted to impose the lineups on him. 
He didn't swallow and they threw him out. 
Then, the Burgos team was 10ª, but it went down.

After a very good season at the Cultural Leonesa, he arrived in Salamanca (2ª B). 
In the first campaign, Hercules took away his promotion. In the second, he stroked him. 
And in the third, another promotion. Lillo became the youngest technician in history in 1ª.

But that was the swan song for the most promising coach of Spanish football. 
Then, he led Oviedo, Tenerife and Zaragoza, but things went wrong: he was dismissed in all three. 
His star went out, but he had the opportunity to perform a triple somersault. 
It was never official, but Lluís Bassat, in the elections for the presidency of Barcelona, had Pep Guardiola and Lillo as coach as sports director. Joan Laporta won.

Neither did the season end nor the City of Murcia, nor in Terrassa (both in 2ª A). From there he made the leap to the Mexican adventure, in the Dorados de Sinaloa.

After a break, the Royal Society, in a clear state of decomposition, sought him to work the miracle of an ascent that escaped him in the last breath of last season. In the present, Lillo has been able to work from the beginning trying to print his personal stamp, always marked by some conditions of the current situation of the club.** "Every war needs warriors," **he says. That's why it varies the systems and rotates the players. He was the _inventor _of the 4-2-3-1, in the early 90s. Now he continues to try to evolve. In the pre-season he used a 1-3-4-3, but he has also bet on the 2-3-2-3, the 4-4-2 in rhombus... And so one weekend yes and another too, although always wanting the protagonist to be the ball.

A revolutionary

Lillo is aware of the impact of his particular way of understanding soccer. **"My philosophy of soccer is my way of life: you play as you live and you play as you feel," **he says. He lives it and feels it in a special way, so it is clear to him that **"e either you adore me, or you hate me."**

In San Sebastián there is a great open debate around the style of play of the Real. In the middle, the _enfant terrible_, the last revolutionary of Spanish football, has returned to the charge.
