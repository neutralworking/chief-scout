

Quick, mobile and combative with a good and accurate range of passing and the energy to go box-to-box, Caicedo’s biggest qualities are his tackling and interceptions. Experienced Dutch team-mate [Joel Veltman](https://theathletic.com/football/player/joel-veltman-EBhmvcwwf06JJRSb/) said: “What I like is he knows where the ball will be. For a midfielder, that’s really good.”

The youngest of 10 children, [[Moises Caicedo]] comes from a poor neighbourhood in Santo Domingo, 100 miles west of Ecuador’s capital city, Quito, where he is known as “El Nino Moi” — the boy Moi. His father owned a rickshaw, his mother sometimes washed clothes to bring extra money into the household.

Ivan Guerra, a local football coach who lived nearby, [paid for his bus fares and food](https://theathletic.com/4670519/2023/07/07/moises-caicedo-brighton-chelsea-he-was-made-for-the-big-clubs/) so that he could train from the age of 13 at the academy of Independiente del Valle (IDV), based in the Quito suburb of Sangolqui.

Progression through the youth ranks to a first-team debut at the age of 17 attracted the attention of clubs in Europe.

[Manchester United](https://theathletic.com/football/team/manchester-united/), Chelsea, [Newcastle United](https://theathletic.com/football/team/newcastle-united/), [West Ham United](https://theathletic.com/football/team/west-ham-united/), [Everton](https://theathletic.com/football/team/everton/), [AC Milan](https://theathletic.com/football/team/milan/), [RB Leipzig](https://theathletic.com/football/team/leipzig/) and Club Bruges were all on the premises, but Brighton had advantages.

They had an existing relationship with IDV, having signed striker Billy Arce in August 2018 from a club who have been members of Ecuador’s top-flight Serie A for the past 13 years, winning the title in 2021.

They had also demonstrated a pathway to first-team football in the [Premier League](https://theathletic.com/football/premier-league/) through their handling of Argentinian future 2022 World Cup winner [Alexis Mac Allister](https://theathletic.com/1942342/2020/07/30/brighton-alexis-mac-allister/), who joined Liverpool this summer. Mac Allister, signed by Brighton as a 20-year-old from Buenos Aires’ Argentinos Juniors in January 2019, had made 18 Premier League appearances by the time Caicedo became an Amex Stadium team-mate at the end of the 2021 January transfer window on a four-and-a-half-year contract.

The fee of around £4million that Brighton paid for the 19-year-old, incorporated a 20 per cent sell-on for IDV. Caicedo’s potential and tactical appreciation of the game were evident then.

Miguel Angel Ramirez, Caicedo’s former manager at IDV, said that he “understands football”, referencing how his reading of the game improved once he reached first-team level and how a “good first touch and good control” enabled him to keep the ball moving.

Caicedo demonstrated some of the assets of his role model growing up, N’Golo Kante, as a deep-lying midfielder dropping between centre-backs to start moves in IDV’s 4-1-4-1 formation, or as one of the more advanced midfielders.

He became a senior international for Ecuador at 18. Coach Gustavo Alfaro handed him the responsibility of marking Lionel Messi in a 1-0 World Cup qualifying defeat by [Argentina](https://theathletic.com/football/team/argentina/), three months before the move to Brighton. Messi scored that day, but only from the penalty spot.

Caicedo’s development, initially under Brighton’s then-head coach Graham Potter, was slow-burning.

His name first appeared on the team sheet nine days after signing, as an unused substitute in a 1-0 defeat away to [Leicester City](https://theathletic.com/football/team/leicester-city/) in the fifth round of the [FA Cup](https://theathletic.com/football/fa-cup/). That became a familiar theme of his first few months, as he was given time to acclimatise to a new country, club and culture, alongside language lessons to improve his limited English.

Caicedo was not listed for a Premier League game until he was again an unused substitute for a 2-1 defeat by Manchester United at Old Trafford that April.

Three further non-playing roles on the bench followed at the end of the 2020-21 season, which started late in mid-September due to the lingering impact of the COVID-19 pandemic extending 2019-20 into August and finished with Brighton in 16th.

The pattern continued at the start of the 2021-22 campaign: Caicedo was an unused substitute in the opening two league matches, finally making his debut in low-key circumstances that month in a 2-0 win away to Championship side [Cardiff City](https://theathletic.com/football/team/cardiff-city/) in the second round of the Carabao Cup.

Caicedo needed match minutes to enhance his progress, so Brighton turned to the loan market.

Identifying a suitable club to place him with, one playing at a good level but not too far away geographically from their base on the south coast of England, was not straightforward. Technical director David Weir, Brighton’s loans manager at the time, said: “The reality with Moises, people won’t believe it now, but we were struggling to find opportunities.”

Brighton were using [TransferRoom, an online marketplace where 650 clubs from across the globe can buy, sell and advertise players](https://theathletic.com/4333076/2023/03/26/inside-transfer-room/) directly and transparently.

TransferRoom’s Plus Pitch tool enables member teams to target up to 20 potential loan clubs. It led to a temporary move for Caicedo in September 2021 to Antwerp-based Beerschot, who were then struggling in the top-tier Belgian Pro League.

Weir said: “Beerschot were at the bottom of the league, they were looking likely to be relegated. And we didn’t have lots of options for Moises. We just had to try to spread our network, to find a solution to enable him to go and play in a strong league to get some minutes on the pitch.

“That was a great learning experience for him. It was difficult for him, living away again. The language barriers and all the things that go with that. But it helped him to develop as a person and as a player.

“We managed that situation, obviously. We take great pride in looking after our players when they are on loan, and [Belgium](https://theathletic.com/football/team/belgium/)’s not too far away, so we can pretty much be in touch with them as well, which was really important.

“It wasn’t a fantastic experience, but for him as a player it was fundamental to his development.”
