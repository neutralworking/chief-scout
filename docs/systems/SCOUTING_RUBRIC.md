# Scouting Rubric — Position-Specific Assessment Guide

> How to evaluate players at each position. Which models matter, what to look for on the pitch, what the data hides, and where players fit in the tactical role system.

---

## GK — Goalkeeper

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: GK (Agility, Footwork, Handling, Reactions). **Secondary**: Commander (organization, vocal presence), Passer (distribution range), Cover (sweeping/reading of danger). The Striker, Dribbler, and Engine models are irrelevant.

### What to Watch
1. **Set position before the shot** — feet together, weight forward, not caught mid-shuffle. Everything else follows from this.
2. **Decision-making on crosses** — does he come and claim, or stay rooted? Watch the half-second hesitation that creates chaos.
3. **Distribution under press** — can he play short with a striker closing? Does he pick the right option or just boot it?
4. **Recovery speed after going to ground** — how quickly he resets for second balls and deflections.
5. **Communication on set pieces** — who organizes the wall? Who calls for the ball at corners? If it's not the keeper, that's a gap.
6. **1v1 body shape** — does he spread and stay big, or dive early and commit? Watch breakaway situations specifically.

### Red Flags
- Punching when he should catch (lack of authority in the area)
- Panicked distribution leading to turnovers in own third
- Poor positioning on near-post shots (coaching can't fully fix this)
- Going quiet when the team is under sustained pressure
- Consistently beaten at the near post from acute angles

### Grade Anchors
**Handling**: 9-10 Courtois, 7-8 Sa, 5-6 Ramsdale.
**Footwork/Distribution**: 9-10 Ederson, 7-8 Alisson, 5-6 Pickford.
**Reactions**: 9-10 Neuer (prime), 7-8 Martinez, 5-6 Sanchez.

### Statistical Traps
- **Save percentage is misleading without shot quality context.** A keeper facing 3 xG from 15 shots looks busier than one facing 3 xG from 5 shots, but the latter has harder saves.
- **Post-shot xG minus goals conceded (PSxG+/-)** is the gold standard, but even this punishes keepers in low-block systems who face fewer but higher-quality chances.
- **Clean sheets correlate with defence quality**, not goalkeeper quality. Ederson behind that City backline vs. a comparable keeper behind Bournemouth — the stat lies.
- Distribution stats (pass completion) reward short passing and punish keepers who attempt long switches. Context is everything.

### Role Suitability
- High GK + Passer → **Libero GK** (Ederson type: plays as an outfielder, high risk/reward distribution)
- High GK + Cover → **Sweeper Keeper** (Neuer type: high defensive line, sweeps space behind)
- High GK + Commander → **Comandante** (Buffon type: organizes, dominates the area vocally)
- High GK + Target → **Shotstopper** (Courtois type: reflexes, presence, shot-stopping specialist)

## WD — Wide Defender (Full-back / Wing-back)

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Engine (Stamina, Pressing, Intensity), Sprinter (Pace, Acceleration). **Secondary**: Passer (Crossing, Pass Range), Cover (Positioning, Interceptions). Dribbler matters for modern attacking full-backs. Controller becomes critical for inverted roles.

### What to Watch
1. **Recovery runs after losing possession high up the pitch** — does he sprint back or jog? This separates professionals from liabilities.
2. **Body shape when defending 1v1** — side-on, jockeying, forcing the winger onto his weak foot? Or flat-footed and diving in?
3. **Timing of overlapping runs** — arriving a half-second too early forces the ball carrier; too late is wasted energy. Watch the relationship with the winger.
4. **Crossing technique under pressure** — can he deliver first-time from a half-yard of space, or does he need to stop, set, and look up?
5. **Positional awareness in a back four vs. back five** — does he naturally tuck in when the ball is on the opposite flank? Watch the blindside.
6. **Decision-making in transition** — when to bomb forward, when to hold. The best full-backs read the game state, not just react.

### Red Flags
- Constantly caught upfield with no recovery pace to compensate
- Can only cross from a standing start (useless against organized defences)
- Loses aerial duels against target wingers (especially on the back post)
- No weak foot — completely predictable going forward
- Gets nutmegged or turned by quick wingers repeatedly (poor 1v1 stance)

### Grade Anchors
**Crossing/Passing**: 9-10 Trent Alexander-Arnold, 7-8 Reece James, 5-6 Ben White.
**Pace**: 9-10 Alphonso Davies, 7-8 Theo Hernandez, 5-6 Azpilicueta.
**Positioning/Cover**: 9-10 Lahm, 7-8 Cancelo, 5-6 Luke Shaw.

### Statistical Traps
- **Crosses per 90 means nothing without cross completion and xA.** A full-back whipping 8 crosses a game into the first defender is not an attacking asset.
- **Tackles won** can be inflated by poor positioning — you make more tackles when you're out of position more often. Robertson's tackle numbers dropped as he improved.
- **Progressive carries** overrate full-backs who dribble into dead ends in the final third. Check whether carries lead to chances.
- Assist numbers for wing-backs in a 3-5-2 are not comparable to full-backs in a flat four — the role is fundamentally different.

### Role Suitability
- High Passer + Dribbler → **Lateral** (TAA type: attacking creator from wide)
- High Engine + Cover → **Fluidificante** (Hakimi type: two-way machine, covers full flank)
- High Controller + Passer → **Invertido** (Cancelo type: inverts into midfield, reads space)
- High Sprinter + Engine → **Corredor** (Davies type: explosive transition runner)

## CD — Centre-back

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Cover (Positioning, Interceptions, Awareness), Destroyer (Tackling, Marking, Clearances), Commander (Leadership, Communication, Concentration). **Secondary**: Passer (for ball-playing CBs), Powerhouse (for aggressive CBs), Target (aerial dominance).

### What to Watch
1. **Positioning before the ball arrives** — is he already in the passing lane, or scrambling to get there? The best CBs are boring to watch because they're always in the right place.
2. **Aerial dominance in both boxes** — winning headers defensively is expected; winning them from set pieces offensively is a weapon. Watch the timing of the jump, not just the height.
3. **Composure on the ball when pressed** — does he play through pressure or panic-clear? Modern football demands this. Watch the first touch under a closing striker.
4. **Partnership intelligence** — who covers when the partner steps out? Who shifts across when the full-back pushes up? This is invisible on highlights.
5. **Recovery pace when beaten** — not raw speed, but the angle of the recovery run. Smart defenders take the inside line; poor ones chase shadows.
6. **Communication** — is he organizing the line, calling offside, pointing to runners? Mute centre-backs cost goals.

### Red Flags
- Steps up to press but leaves a gap that isn't covered (no partnership awareness)
- Consistently beaten in foot races when the line is high (system mismatch, not fixable)
- Poor weak foot under pressure (gives the ball away in build-up)
- Heads the ball downward under pressure (should be heading for distance/width)
- Loses concentration in the 75th-90th minute window (check late goals conceded)

### Grade Anchors
**Positioning/Cover**: 9-10 Van Dijk, 7-8 Ruben Dias, 5-6 Tarkowski.
**Tackling/Destroyer**: 9-10 Chiellini (prime), 7-8 Kounde, 5-6 Maguire.
**Passing/Build-up**: 9-10 Stones, 7-8 Saliba, 5-6 Mings.

### Statistical Traps
- **Tackles per 90 rewards CBs in bad teams.** A centre-back in a dominant possession side will barely tackle — that's a feature, not a bug.
- **Aerial duel win %** is useful but context-dependent. A CB who wins 60% of 8 aerial duels per game is under more pressure than one winning 75% of 3.
- **Pass completion % is inflated by short sideways passes.** Look at progressive passing distance and passes into the final third specifically.
- **Clean sheets** are a team stat. Evaluate individual contribution through errors leading to goals/shots and defensive actions in the build-up to chances conceded.

### Role Suitability
- High Passer + Cover → **Libero** (Stones type: steps into midfield, progressive on the ball)
- High Cover + Controller → **Sweeper** (Hummels type: reads danger, covers space behind the line)
- High Commander + Destroyer → **Zagueiro** (Van Dijk type: vocal, dominant, sets the tone)
- High Powerhouse + Destroyer → **Stopper** (Konate type: front-foot, aggressive, wins physical duels)

## DM — Defensive Midfielder

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Cover (Positioning, Interceptions, Awareness, Discipline), Controller (Anticipation, Composure, Decisions, Tempo). **Secondary**: Destroyer (Tackling, Marking), Passer (for playmaking DMs), Powerhouse (for ball-winning DMs). Engine matters in high-press systems.

### What to Watch
1. **Shadow covering** — watch where the DM positions himself when the ball is in wide areas. The best ones are already blocking the cutback lane. This is the single most important thing.
2. **Body orientation when receiving the ball** — open body, half-turned, scanning. A DM who receives the ball facing his own goal and has to turn is a liability against the press.
3. **Tempo control** — does he speed the game up when the team needs urgency, and slow it down when they need control? This is the metronome function. Watch ball-holding under pressure.
4. **Screening the back four** — does he sit or does he chase? A DM who leaves his post to press a midfielder 30 yards away has abandoned his job.
5. **Passing lane selection under pressure** — the difference between a recycled possession and a chance created. Watch the third-man pass especially.
6. **Foul discipline** — intelligent fouling in the right areas vs. reckless challenges. Rodri's foul map is surgical; others are chaotic.

### Red Flags
- Gets drawn out of position by the ball (ball-watching, not scanning)
- Can only pass sideways or backward (limits the team's progression)
- Picks up avoidable yellow cards in the first 30 minutes (limits aggression for 60+ minutes)
- Poor turning circle — gets pressed and loses possession in dangerous areas
- No aerial presence (exploitable from long balls over the top or direct play)

### Grade Anchors
**Positioning/Cover**: 9-10 Rodri, 7-8 Declan Rice, 5-6 Guimaraes.
**Passing/Controller**: 9-10 Busquets (prime), 7-8 Jorginho, 5-6 McTominay.
**Tackling/Destroyer**: 9-10 Kante (prime), 7-8 Caicedo, 5-6 Bissouma.

### Statistical Traps
- **Interceptions per 90 rewards proactive readers**, but can also indicate a DM who wanders from his zone. Cross-reference with positional heatmaps.
- **Pass completion % is meaningless for DMs without progressive pass data.** A DM completing 94% of 5-yard passes is not comparable to one at 87% attempting line-breaking passes.
- **Tackles + interceptions combined** is the lazy scout's metric. A great DM prevents passes from being attempted — his influence is invisible in event data.
- **Pressures** overrate aggressive DMs and underrate positional ones. Busquets was the best DM of his generation with modest pressure numbers.

### Role Suitability
- High Passer + Controller → **Regista** (Pirlo type: deep creator, dictates play with passing range)
- High Cover + Destroyer → **Anchor** (Makelele type: pure shield, intercepts, guards the space)
- High Controller + Cover → **Pivote** (Rodri type: the brain, organizes shape, reads everything)
- High Powerhouse + Destroyer → **Volante** (Kante type: aggressive ball-winner, disrupts and recycles)

## CM — Central Midfielder

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Engine (Stamina, Pressing, Intensity, Versatility), Controller (Decisions, Tempo, Composure). **Secondary**: Passer (for creative CMs), Sprinter (for dynamic CMs), Creator (for advanced playmaking CMs). Destroyer matters in double-pivot setups. Striker model relevant for goal-scoring midfielders.

### What to Watch
1. **Third-man runs** — the run nobody tracks. When the ball goes wide, does the CM arrive at the edge of the box? This is the Lampard/Bellingham trait and it separates 7s from 9s.
2. **Receiving between the lines** — can he find pockets of space between the opposition midfield and defence? Watch the body shape: half-turn, first touch forward, already scanning.
3. **Defensive transitions** — what happens the instant possession is lost? Does he press immediately (counter-press) or drop into shape? Both are valid; the wrong one for the system is the problem.
4. **Range of passing** — not just long balls, but the ability to switch play, thread through-balls, and play simple when simple is right. Versatility of passing, not just volume.
5. **Ball retention under pressure** — can he hold the ball in traffic? Shielding, body strength, tight turning. A CM who gets dispossessed in the middle third kills transitions.
6. **Game management in the final 15 minutes** — does he take the sting out when leading? Does he push when chasing? Football intelligence shows most clearly when tired.

### Red Flags
- Goes missing for 20-minute stretches (low off-ball movement, no scanning)
- Only effective in one phase — can attack but not defend, or vice versa
- No goal threat from central areas (predictable, easy to mark)
- Slows down significantly after 60 minutes (stamina issue kills the role)
- Avoids receiving under pressure (hides behind opponents instead of showing for the ball)

### Grade Anchors
**Engine/Box-to-box**: 9-10 Bellingham, 7-8 Barella, 5-6 Gallagher.
**Passing/Creativity**: 9-10 Kroos, 7-8 Pedri, 5-6 Neves.
**Stamina/Work rate**: 9-10 Kante (prime), 7-8 Valverde, 5-6 Dewsbury-Hall.

### Statistical Traps
- **Chances created is misleading for CMs** because a set-piece taker gets cheap volume. Always separate open-play chance creation.
- **Touches in the opposition box** is the real goal-threat indicator, but it's system-dependent. A CM in a 4-3-3 with a deep role will never match a 3-4-1-2 mezzala.
- **Distance covered** doesn't distinguish useful running from aimless jogging. High-speed sprints and sprint frequency are better intensity indicators.
- **xG+xA per 90** overrates CMs in attacking systems and underrates those doing invisible work (breaking lines, recycling, winning second balls).

### Role Suitability
- High Passer + Creator → **Mezzala** (Kovacic type: half-space operator, technical in tight areas)
- High Engine + Cover → **Tuttocampista** (Bellingham type: all-pitch runner, arrives in both boxes)
- High Controller + Passer → **Metodista** (Kroos type: orchestrates rhythm, metronomic passing)
- High Sprinter + Engine → **Relayeur** (Valverde type: pace and power to break lines and link phases)

## WM — Wide Midfielder

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Dribbler (Take-ons, Skills, First Touch, Carries), Sprinter (Pace, Acceleration, Movement). **Secondary**: Passer (Crossing, Through Balls), Engine (Stamina, Pressing — especially for two-way wide mids), Creator (Vision, Unpredictability for playmaking wingers).

### What to Watch
1. **1v1 success rate in the final third** — not just attempted take-ons, but successful ones that lead to crosses or shots. A winger who beats his man but then over-hits the cross has done half a job.
2. **Movement off the ball when the team has possession centrally** — does he pin the full-back wide to create space, or drift inside and clog the middle? Understanding of width is fundamental.
3. **Defensive contribution when the full-back is beaten** — does he track back to cover? In modern football, a winger who won't defend is a luxury most teams can't afford.
4. **Delivery quality** — early crosses from the byline, cutbacks, in-swinging crosses. Watch the variety, not just the volume. One type of cross is predictable.
5. **Ability to play on both flanks** — can he be effective on either side? Right-footed on the right (touchline) vs. right-footed on the left (cutting inside) are entirely different roles.
6. **End product under fatigue** — wingers often fade after 60 minutes. Does the decision-making deteriorate, or just the pace?

### Red Flags
- Predictable dribbling pattern (always goes one way, defenders read it)
- Disappears against deep blocks (can only play in transition, not against parked buses)
- Zero defensive work rate (opposing full-back has a free run every time)
- Poor end product despite impressive dribbling stats (lots of tricks, no output)
- Needs space to be effective (can't operate in tight areas or half-spaces)

### Grade Anchors
**Dribbling**: 9-10 Saka, 7-8 Diaby, 5-6 Neto.
**Crossing/Delivery**: 9-10 Beckham (peak), 7-8 Kulusevski, 5-6 Rashford.
**Pace**: 9-10 Adama Traore, 7-8 Chiesa, 5-6 Bernardo Silva.

### Statistical Traps
- **Successful dribbles per 90 rewards volume over quality.** A winger who attempts 10 and completes 5 in his own half is worse than one who completes 3 in the final third.
- **xA (expected assists) punishes wingers who create for poor finishers.** Cross-reference with shot-creating actions for a fuller picture.
- **Heatmaps that show wide-only presence aren't necessarily bad** — it depends on the system. A touchline winger in a 4-3-3 should be out there. An inside forward shouldn't.
- **Goal contributions (G+A)** flatten the difference between a winger who scores 12 and assists 3 vs. one who scores 3 and assists 12. These are completely different profiles.

### Role Suitability
- High Dribbler + Passer → **Winger** (Saka type: beats men, delivers quality crosses and final balls)
- High Engine + Cover → **Tornante** (Kostic type: covers full flank, selfless two-way runner)
- High Controller + Cover → **False Winger** (Bernardo Silva type: drifts inside intelligently, creates overloads)
- High Sprinter + Engine → **Shuttler** (Chiesa type: raw pace to stretch defences end to end)

## AM — Attacking Midfielder (No. 10)

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Creator (Vision, Creativity, Unpredictability, Guile), Controller (Decisions, Composure, Tempo). **Secondary**: Dribbler (for ball-carrying 10s), Striker (shooting for goal-scoring 10s), Sprinter (movement for mobile 10s). Engine matters for pressing 10s.

### What to Watch
1. **Receiving on the half-turn** — the defining skill of a No. 10. Can he receive facing forward between the lines? Watch the body position as the ball arrives; the best ones have already scanned and know what's on.
2. **Weight of the final pass** — not just direction, but pace. A through-ball played too hard is a goalkeeper's; too soft is a defender's. The margins are centimetres. Watch especially on breaking runs.
3. **Movement to find space** — does he drift laterally to find pockets, or stand in a fixed zone waiting for the ball? The best 10s are impossible to mark because they're never where you expect.
4. **Pressing triggers** — in modern systems, the 10 initiates the press. Does he press with intelligence (cutting passing lanes) or just chase the ball?
5. **Performance in big games** — the 10 role is where mentality shows. Some players dominate against bottom-half sides and vanish in derbies. Track output by opponent strength.

### Red Flags
- Can only play with the ball at feet (no off-ball intelligence, no runs behind)
- Slows the attack down by over-dribbling in central areas
- Zero defensive contribution (leaves the midfield exposed in transition)
- Inconsistent — brilliant one week, invisible the next (mentality issue)
- Can't play in a midfield three (too defensive a liability to drop deeper)
- Poor composure in the final third (snatches at shots, overhits passes when it matters)

### Grade Anchors
**Vision/Creativity**: 9-10 De Bruyne, 7-8 Odegaard, 5-6 Maddison.
**Dribbling/Ball-carrying**: 9-10 Messi, 7-8 Grealish, 5-6 Eze.
**Shooting/Goal threat**: 9-10 Bruno Fernandes, 7-8 Havertz, 5-6 Bowen.

### Statistical Traps
- **Key passes per 90 rewards volume, not quality.** A 10 who plays 5 low-quality key passes is less dangerous than one who plays 2 that slice open a defence.
- **xA is the best chance-creation metric**, but it still doesn't capture the pass before the assist (hockey assists). Some 10s are two passes from the goal — their influence is upstream.
- **Goals + assists combined** hides whether the 10 is a scorer or a creator. These require different supporting casts. A scoring 10 needs creative wingers; a creative 10 needs clinical forwards.
- **Touches in the final third** can be inflated by sideways possession in non-threatening areas. Progressive passes received is a better indicator of genuine between-the-lines involvement.

### Role Suitability
- High Dribbler + Creator → **Trequartista** (Zidane type: free-roaming genius, dribbles and creates at will)
- High Engine + Striker → **Seconda Punta** (Griezmann type: links play through movement, arrives to score)
- High Controller + Creator → **Enganche** (Riquelme type: the hook, sees everything, orchestrates from a standing start)
- High Sprinter + Striker → **Boxcrasher** (Bruno Fernandes type: dynamic runs into the box, arrives with pace and power)

## WF — Wide Forward

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Dribbler (Take-ons, Carries, First Touch), Sprinter (Pace, Acceleration, Movement), Striker (Shooting — Short Range, Mid Range). **Secondary**: Creator (for playmaking wide forwards), Engine (for pressing forwards). The difference between WF and WM is that Striker models matter far more here.

### What to Watch
1. **Runs in behind vs. runs to feet** — the balance between these defines the player. A wide forward who only wants to feet is predictable; one who only runs in behind can't function against deep blocks. Watch the variety.
2. **Finishing from the cut-inside position** — the signature move of the modern inside forward. Can he finish curling onto the far post, or is it always straight at the keeper? Check the conversion rate on these specific chances.
3. **Interaction with the full-back and striker** — does the WF create 2v1s with the full-back on the overlap? Does he combine with the CF through quick one-twos? Partnership play is what makes a wide forward dangerous, not isolation.
4. **Pressing from the front** — the first line of defence. Does he press with a plan (angling the press to force play into traps) or just chase?
5. **Composure in 1v1 with the goalkeeper** — wide forwards get more 1v1s than any other position. Finishing quality here is the difference between 15 and 25 goals a season.
6. **Positional discipline in the defensive phase** — does he track the opposing full-back, or leave the winger/wing-back exposed?

### Red Flags
- Shoots from every angle regardless of the option (selfish, low football IQ)
- Can only function in transition (useless against low blocks)
- Loses the ball in the final third trying to beat one too many (diminishing returns on dribbling)
- No weak foot at all (defenders just show him onto it)
- Gets bullied by aggressive full-backs (too slight, no shielding ability)

### Grade Anchors
**Finishing**: 9-10 Salah, 7-8 Son, 5-6 Diaz.
**Dribbling/Carrying**: 9-10 Vinicius Jr, 7-8 Saka, 5-6 Rashford.
**Movement**: 9-10 Muller (prime), 7-8 Mane, 5-6 Martinelli.

### Statistical Traps
- **Goals per 90 is crude** — always use npxG per 90 (non-penalty expected goals) to strip out penalty inflation. Some "20-goal" wide forwards have 7 penalties.
- **Shot volume overrates players who shoot from bad positions.** Shots inside the box per 90 and shot quality (xG per shot) are far better indicators.
- **xG overperformance is unsustainable long-term** for most players. If a WF scores 20 from 12 xG one season, expect regression — unless they're Salah or Messi, who genuinely overperform their xG consistently through finishing skill.
- **Carry progressive distance** can be inflated by long diagonal runs in transition that lead nowhere. Filter for carries ending in a shot or key pass.

### Role Suitability
- High Dribbler + Sprinter → **Inverted Winger** (Salah type: cuts inside on strong foot, shoots or creates)
- High Engine + Striker → **Raumdeuter** (Muller type: interprets space, arrives in scoring positions without the ball)
- High Creator + Dribbler → **Inventor** (Neymar type: creates something from nothing, vision from wide areas)
- High Sprinter + Striker → **Extremo** (Mbappe type: electric pace stretches defences, direct and devastating)

## CF — Centre-forward

### Key Attributes
**Primary**: Striker (Short Range, Mid Range, Long Range, Penalties), Target (Aerial Duels, Heading, Jumping). **Secondary**: Sprinter (Movement, Pace for mobile strikers), Creator (for false 9 types), Engine (for pressing CFs), Powerhouse (hold-up play for target men).

### What to Watch
1. **Movement in the 6-yard box** — this is where goals live. Watch the striker's positioning 2 seconds before the ball arrives. Does he lose his marker? Does he anticipate where the cross will go? Haaland's movement in the 6-yard box is the best in world football because he reads the delivery and gets ahead of the defender.
2. **First touch under pressure from a centre-back** — can he control a lofted ball with a defender on his shoulder and keep possession? This is the foundation of hold-up play and it separates levels.
3. **Finishing variety** — left foot, right foot, head, volleys, placed, driven. A striker with one finish is easier to defend. Check goal breakdowns across seasons.
4. **Runs that stretch defences even when the ball doesn't come** — the run itself creates space for others. Great strikers make 30 runs for every ball they receive. Evaluate the work, not just the reward.
5. **Link play** — does the CF bring others into the game? Layoffs, flick-ons, holding the ball and waiting for runners. Especially important in single-striker systems.
6. **Penalty box instincts** — rebounds, deflections, scrappy goals. The best strikers score ugly goals because they anticipate chaos.

### Red Flags
- Drops too deep too often (leaves no one in the box for crosses — a false 9 can do this, but a No. 9 shouldn't)
- Poor aerial presence when the team needs a Plan B (can't win headers from set pieces or direct play)
- Goes offside constantly (poor timing, not poor pace — this is a decision-making issue)
- Sulks when service is poor (mentality red flag — the best strikers create their own chances)
- No pressing contribution (modern football requires CFs to press; one who doesn't is a tactical burden)

### Grade Anchors
**Finishing**: 9-10 Haaland, 7-8 Isak, 5-6 Richarlison.
**Movement**: 9-10 Lewandowski, 7-8 Watkins, 5-6 Toney.
**Hold-up/Link play**: 9-10 Kane, 7-8 Giroud, 5-6 Antonio.

### Statistical Traps
- **Goals per 90 is the laziest metric in football.** A striker scoring 0.5 G/90 from 0.7 xG/90 is underperforming. One scoring 0.5 from 0.3 xG is world-class. Always use xG as the baseline.
- **Aerial duel win % is deceptive for strikers** — a target man who wins 50% of 10 aerial duels per game is far more valuable than one winning 70% of 3, because volume creates second balls and chaos.
- **Minutes per goal** penalizes strikers who contribute massively to build-up. Kane's minutes-per-goal was never elite, but his overall contribution (xA, progressive passes, build-up involvement) puts him in a different tier.
- **Big chances missed** is an emotionally loaded stat. All elite strikers miss big chances because they get into big-chance positions more often. It's a volume indicator, not a quality one.

### Role Suitability
- High Striker + Dribbler → **Poacher** (Haaland type: pure finisher, movement and instinct in the box)
- High Engine + Destroyer → **Spearhead** (Vardy type: leads the press, relentless work rate, runs in behind)
- High Creator + Controller → **Falso Nove** (Messi type: drops deep, pulls CBs out, creates for runners)
- High Target + Powerhouse → **Prima Punta** (Giroud type: aerial dominance, hold-up, physical reference point)
